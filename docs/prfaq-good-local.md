# Press Release

## Good Local Launches on the Upper Delaware to Turn Supporting River Towns Into a <em>Game Everyone Wins</em>

*One passport where locals, weekenders, and river visitors earn rewards at the independent cafés, inns, outfitters, shops, and galleries they keep coming back to — and where small-town businesses get a real loyalty program of their own, plus a region that markets itself together.*

**BARRYVILLE, N.Y. — June 30, 2026 —** Today, at the height of river season, Good Local launched across the Upper Delaware region — Barryville, Narrowsburg, Callicoon, and the towns within 40 miles of the river — starting from a founding cluster in Narrowsburg. Patrons add the free Upper Delaware Passport to their phone's wallet at any participating register — no app to download — and earn a stamp every visit. Stamps unlock perks at the businesses they frequent and milestones across the region, and the passport's recommendations come from one source only: where verified regulars actually return. Each business runs its own rewards program — its own perks, its own promotions, its own customer dashboard — for $79 a month, working from the day it signs up, whether one business is on the network or seventy.

River-town businesses live and die on a short season and a small base of regulars, and they lose both to leaks they can't see. Acquiring a new customer costs five to twenty-five times more than retaining an existing one, yet most small-town businesses have no retention tool beyond a paper punch card that gets lost in a wallet. Hundreds of thousands of people float the Upper Delaware each season — then drive home having visited one outfitter and one restaurant, never learning the bookstore, the gallery, or the inn existed. Weekenders who own homes here split their spending with the city and discover local spots by accident, over years. And the average American already belongs to more than 17 loyalty programs and actively uses about half — each one a separate card or app for a single store. Each business markets alone, on a budget of a few hundred dollars a month, hoping the next visitor stumbles in.

Good Local gives each business its own modern loyalty program first — stamps per visit, perks it designs and funds itself, and a dashboard that finally shows who's coming back and what brings them in. Then it connects those programs into something no single business could build: one passport for the whole region. A visitor who checks in at the outfitter after a canoe trip sees where verified regulars go next — the café in Barryville, the gallery in Narrowsburg, the bakery in Callicoon — so the outfitter's one-day customer becomes every other business's prospect before they drive home. A weekender's fifth visit to the same café unlocks the regular's pour the owner chose to offer. No points flying between businesses, no business funding a competitor's rewards — every perk belongs to the business that offers it, and the region's gravity does the rest.

"Every business owner on this river told us the same two things: 'I need my regulars back one more time a month, and I need the people who float past my door to actually walk through it,'" said [Founder Name], CEO of Good Local. "So we built both: your own loyalty program that works the day you sign up, and a passport that turns the whole corridor into one front door. A river town can't out-market a chain — but a region acting together can out-welcome anyone."

Getting started is simple. Businesses sign up at goodlocal.app/business, design a perk, print their QR kit, and are live the same day. Patrons tap the QR at the register, add the passport to their wallet, and earn their first stamp on the spot.

"We bought our place up here three years ago, and it still took us two of them to find half these spots," said Maya Reyes, a Brooklyn-based weekender with a house outside Barryville. "Now I'm three stamps from the regular's shelf at the bookstore, the passport shows me where people who actually live here keep going back, and we've been to four places this month we'd driven past for three years. It makes being a regular feel like something."

To join, visit goodlocal.app — no download required.

# FAQ

## External FAQ (customers & press)

**Q: What is Good Local and how does it work?**
A: Good Local is a free regional passport for discovering and supporting independent local businesses. Tap the QR code at any participating register, add the Upper Delaware Passport to your phone's wallet, and earn a stamp each visit. Stamps unlock perks at the businesses you frequent — each business designs its own — plus milestones across the region. Recommendations are ranked by verified return visits from locals and regulars, never by paid placement.

**Q: Do I need to download an app?**
A: No. The passport lives in Apple/Google Wallet, added straight from the register QR. A web app at goodlocal.app handles discovery and your stamp history. If you'd rather not scan anything, give the register your phone number — staff can log your visit.

**Q: What does it cost?**
A: Free for patrons, always. Businesses pay **$79/month from day one**, no long-term contract, with an optional $49/month winter tier November–April. Founding members lock the $79 rate and get launch-marketing placement. What a business gets on day one: its own complete rewards program — stamps, perks, promotions, printable QR kit, and a repeat-customer dashboard — working immediately, regardless of how many other businesses have joined.

**Q: Can I redeem stamps earned at one business somewhere else?**
A: No — and that's deliberate. Stamps build your passport, and perks belong to the business that offers them, so no business ever funds a competitor's rewards. Your passport still works everywhere: one identity, one stamp history, regional milestones, and discovery across every participating business.

**Q: Where is it available?**
A: The Upper Delaware region — everywhere within roughly 40 miles of Barryville, NY, including Narrowsburg, Callicoon, Eldred, Jeffersonville, Livingston Manor, and the Honesdale–Hawley–Milford corridor in Pennsylvania. Launch day starts with a founding cluster in Narrowsburg, growing across the region all season. New regions open only when a committed anchor cluster exists.

**Q: How are recommendations ranked? Can businesses pay for placement?**
A: No paid placement, ever. Places are ranked by verified return visits — how often real people actually come back. In the founding season, while that data accrues, the app shows hand-curated founding picks plus a live "verified regulars this season" counter for each business; the computed ranking switches on once there's enough real data to be honest.

**Q: How is my data and privacy handled?**
A: Your visit history is yours. Businesses see aggregate patterns for their own establishment (visit frequency, which perks work), never your individual history across other businesses. Location is used only when you check in — no passive tracking. We do not sell personal data to anyone, including participating businesses.

**Q: What happens if a business closes or leaves?**
A: Your passport, stamps, and regional milestones are unaffected. Perks specific to that business end with it.

**Q: Can I pay through the app?**
A: Not at launch. In-app payments are planned for a later phase; check-ins are the core of season one.

**Q: How do I get support?**
A: help@goodlocal.app for patrons; every founding business gets a dedicated onboarding contact.

## Internal FAQ (the hard questions)

### Customer & demand

**Q: Who exactly is the customer?**
A: The paying customer is the **independent owner-operator** ($200k–$2M/yr, marketing budget under $1k/month) in the Upper Delaware region — 150–250 of them. The beachhead patron is the **NYC weekender/second-home owner**: QR-fluent, 2–8 visits per season, identity-motivated ("be a regular here") — sized honestly at low-single-thousands of reachable households around the founding cluster. Locals and day visitors ride along; the staff-entered phone-number path exists so QR-averse year-round locals can carry the October–April shoulder.

**Q: What proof do we have the problem is real?**
A: Verified at two levels. The retention problem: 5–25× acquisition-vs-retention economics, >1/3 of operators dissatisfied with loyalty tools, and complaint-record evidence of owners burned by $200–300/mo loyalty SaaS with opaque ROI. The discovery gap: nobody has ranked places by verified return visits since Foursquare exited consumers (Dec 2024). What remains unproven is the **mechanism** — that weekenders will check in, return, and let the app steer new visits. That is assumption A1, the named riskiest assumption, with zero first-hand evidence; the launch is its test, with a pre-registered four-part kill condition (below).

**Q: Why charge $79 from day one instead of a free founding season?**
A: Founder decision (June 6, 2026), and the evidence cuts both ways — recorded honestly. **For:** unpaid pilots convert poorly (~95% wasted per SaaStr; paid pilots convert ~3.2× better); SCVA's free membership already anchors the region toward $0, and a free season would deepen that; day-one billing pulls the cash willingness-to-pay signal forward into the June founding push — 15 businesses *paying* $79 is far stronger evidence than 15 signatures. **Against (accepted):** it raises the June 20 Mel-gate difficulty, and risks the Belly failure mode if density stalls. **What makes it coherent:** the standalone-first architecture — on day one a business buys a complete working loyalty program for itself (a product Loopy charges $25–69 for, here with a better dashboard), not a seat in an empty network. The network is compounding upside, not the thing being paid for. Mitigations: founding-rate lock, $49 winter tier, and the pre-gate stands — under 10 paying signups by June 20 moves the date.

**Q: Would customers change behavior to adopt this?**
A: Businesses barely change anything (QR by the register, one perk, same-day setup) — and now have classic price-for-value reasoning instead of a free-trial decision. Patrons must adopt wallet-pass check-ins: breadth behavior (try a 2nd business) is well-supported by passport analogs; **depth behavior (return to the same business) is the honest unknown** — DMO trail passports show one-and-done collection (0.29–1.3 check-ins/user), which is exactly why the kill condition carries collection-toy detectors.

**Q: How will we test and measure success?**
A: Season-one gates, anchored to comparables: **Breadth** ≥40% of one-time check-in patrons reach a 2nd business within 21 days (target, not floor). **Depth** (the renewal driver): same-business repeat-visit rate ≥15% and median check-ins per active patron ≥2. **Steered discovery:** ≥25% of active (2+ check-in) patrons make a verified first visit the app steered. **Volume:** 30–50 patron signups per founding business → 250–500 active patrons by Labor Day (2,000 is a year-2 stretch). **Commercial:** ≥15 paying founding businesses by June 20; ≥60% still paying (or on winter tier) November 1. **Kill/pivot the mechanic** if, by August 15 among ≥200 actives: 2nd-business rate <20%, steered rate <10%, median check-ins <2, or same-business repeat <15%. Sample rule: <500 passport adds by July 31 → the read is inconclusive (extend or widen), never pass or fail. All gates ride on the check-in trust model — rotating QR + per-device daily rate limits — or they're void.

### Market & economics

**Q: What is the TAM? Show the math.**
A: Region first: 150–250 addressable independents × $948/yr ≈ **$140–240k regional ARR ceiling**, before the winter-tier blend. National frame (unchanged from v1, verified): ~1.5M US independents in our verticals × $948 ≈ $1.4B SaaS ceiling; a realistic 10-year capture of ~2% ≈ **$30M ARR**. Venture-scale only if the region playbook repeats; a fine bootstrap business if it doesn't. Full scenario math: `docs/earnings-model.md`.

**Q: What are the unit economics?**
A: ~90% gross margin on SaaS ($79 revenue vs ~$8/mo marginal cost). Region launch cost ~$60–90k to reach ~75 businesses ≈ $60–71k ARR/region → **~12–15-month payback per region** — now partially offset by day-one billing (~$4–6k collected in season one from the founding cluster alone). Mel's relationships make region #1 cheaper than the model; the honest question is whether region #2 has a Mel — which is why "recruit a second local champion" is a season-one deliverable.

**Q: What upfront investment is required, and who funds the runway?**
A: Phase 1 (this season): ~$50–100k — lean build of the check-in MVP, hardware, launch. Phase 2 (payments, hardened ledger, region playbook): ~$800k–1.2M over the following 12 months if season-one gates pass. **A12 — the runway question (mandatory):** day-one billing produces real but small revenue (~$15–20k in year one); it does not fund the build. The ~11 months to material revenue must be funded at planned burn, and the number gets written down before any code.

### Competition & approach

**Q: What do customers use today, and why are we better?**
A: Paper punch cards (no data), Square Loyalty $45–49 (POS-locked, siloed), Toast ~$185 add-on (contracts), Fivestars/SumUp ~$299 (complaint epicenter, forced annual contracts in 2024), and Loopy Loyalty $25–95 (digital stamp cards, including a multi-merchant community card). **The honest price defense at $79:** the stamp loop is commoditized — what Loopy doesn't have is the repeat-customer dashboard, the verified-regulars discovery ranking, the regional passport gravity, and a local team onboarding the region's businesses one relationship at a time. The Mel Test doubles as the check: founding owners must be buying the dashboard and the network, not the stamps.

**Q: Should we build, buy, or partner?**
A: Build the passport, dashboard, and trust model. Partner for payments later (Stripe; building rails is fatal scope creep). Treat SCVA as a **complement, not a sponsor**: it runs a free brochure-style app and sells merchant analytics — we hold the verified-return-visit data it lacks. One meeting now for a letter of support and co-marketing; grant/sponsorship money is a 2027 track (next application window ~Nov 2026, awarded ~Jan 2027 — after our kill gate, so the bet never depends on it).

**Q: Why won't Square or Toast just build this?**
A: They could build cross-merchant loyalty tomorrow, but their incentive is per-merchant SaaS expansion inside their own POS ecosystems — a community network requires a neutral aggregator, which is structurally not them. No POS incumbent has announced one as of June 2026. Estimated window: 3–5 years; our defense is region-level density, the community brand, and the regulars data.

### Feasibility & dependencies

**Q: What must we build, and what's hard?**
A: Four things: (1) the **standalone business program** — stamps per visit, perk management, QR kit, dashboard — simple, and it's the day-one product; (2) the **check-in trust model** — rotating QR codes + per-device per-business daily rate limits; every metric, perk, and ranking rides on check-ins being true (and the legal memo covers anti-gaming); (3) the **cold-start ranking policy** — no computed ranking until the data is honest; curated founding picks + live regulars counters in season one; (4) the **perk fairness governance** — each business funds only its own perks, with design guardrails so perks are incremental visits, not cannibalized margin (promo cannibalization runs 30–50% when unmanaged).

**Q: What do we depend on?**
A: Apple/Google Wallet (distribution — the wallet-vs-PWA A/B resolves the issuance path), a thin web app, and — most decisive — **Mel**. Mel's relationships are the entire region-#1 wedge and a named single point of failure: the September interviews ask "would you renew if Mel weren't involved?", and recruiting a second local champion is an explicit season-one deliverable.

### Risk & scope

**Q: What are the top 3 reasons this fails?**
A: (1) **Depth never materializes** — patrons collect businesses like trail badges (the DMO-passport pattern: 0.29–1.3 check-ins/user) instead of becoming regulars; detected by the median-check-ins and same-business-repeat kill thresholds on August 15. (2) **Winter** — the region empties October–April, and the only patrons physically present are the segment we deprioritized; mitigations: the $49 winter tier, winter perks aimed at locals and weekenders, the staff-entered check-in path — and now a November 1 retention gate (≥60% of founding businesses still paying) that gives winter churn an early, explicit number. (3) **Day-one price stalls density** — $79 against a 14-day founding push underperforms, the cluster launches thin, and the network layer never compounds; detected by the June 20 pre-gate, mitigated by the standalone-first pitch (the program is worth $79 alone) and the founding-rate lock. Legal/regulatory: closed-loop, no-cash-value stamps minimize NY/PA stored-value and escheatment exposure — confirmed in writing by counsel before launch, with anti-gaming folded into the same memo.

**Q: What are we explicitly NOT doing in v1?**
A: No shared currency or cross-business redemption (Phase-2 experiment, gated on a network-funded-credit design with a real liability holder). No computed discovery ranking until the data supports it. No in-app payments at launch. No paid placement, ever (trust posture — flagged honestly as an untested revenue ceiling). No POS integration or replacement. No native apps at launch (wallet pass + web first). No multi-region anything. No chains or franchises. No crypto, no tokens.

**Q: What's the most uncomfortable honest answer in this document?**
A: Three, now. First: the season may prove patrons love collecting places but won't become regulars anywhere — in which case the depth gates kill the mechanic by August 15 and the surviving company is a per-business loyalty SaaS in a price war with Loopy. Second: region #1's advantages — Mel, the river, the tight Narrowsburg cluster — may be exactly why it works here and nowhere else; a great season proves the Upper Delaware loves Good Local, not that the playbook travels. Third: charging $79 from day one is the founder's bet against the discovery's recommendation — if the June 20 gate fails, we'll know in 14 days whether the price or the product was the problem, and the date (not the scope) absorbs the damage. All three are knowable for under $100k and one season. That is exactly what this document is for.
